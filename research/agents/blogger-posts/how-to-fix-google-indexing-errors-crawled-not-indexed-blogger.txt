TITLE: Fix Google Indexing Errors: Crawled Not Indexed and Discovered Not Indexed

<p><em>Originally published at <a href="https://www.praveentechworld.com/blog/how-to-fix-google-indexing-errors-crawled-not-indexed">https://www.praveentechworld.com</a>.</em></p>
<p>If you have ever checked Google Search Console and seen "Crawled - currently not indexed" or "Discovered - currently not indexed" next to your URLs, you know the frustration. Google knows the page exists. It visited the page. But it decided not to include it in search results.</p>
<p>These two errors are the most common index coverage issues on Search Console. They are also the most misunderstood.</p>
<h2>What Each Status Actually Means</h2>
<p><strong>Discovered - currently not indexed</strong> means Google found your URL (probably through your sitemap or a link) but has not tried to crawl it yet. The page is in Google's queue waiting for a crawl slot.</p>
<p>This typically happens when:</p>
<ul>
<li>Your site has many pages and Google is pacing its crawls</li>
<li>Your site is new with limited crawl budget</li>
<li>Google found the URL recently and has not gotten to it yet</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Crawled - currently not indexed</strong> means Googlebot visited the page, read its content, but decided not to add it to the index. This is more serious because Google actively chose to skip your page.</p>
<p>Common causes include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Thin or low-quality content</li>
<li>Duplicate content that adds nothing new</li>
<li>Technical issues that make the page appear broken to Googlebot</li>
<li>Insufficient internal links signaling the page is important</li>
</ul>
<p>If you are new to Search Console, the <a href="https://www.praveentechworld.com/blog/how-to-enable-google-search-console-for-your-website">GSC setup guide</a> covers how to verify your site and submit your sitemap.</p>
<h2>Step 1: Check If the Page Should Be Indexed</h2>
<p>Before troubleshooting, ask yourself: should this page be in Google's index?</p>
<p>Pages that Google typically excludes on purpose:</p>
<ul>
<li>Thin affiliate pages with no original content</li>
<li>Tag or category pages that list the same content as other pages</li>
<li>Pages with noindex tags (check your HTML for <code>&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex"&gt;</code>)</li>
<li>Pages blocked by robots.txt</li>
<li>Duplicate versions of other pages</li>
</ul>
<p>If your page falls into one of these categories, the fix is not technical — it is strategic. Either improve the page to justify indexing, or accept that Google is making the right call.</p>
<h2>Step 2: Use the URL Inspection Tool</h2>
<p>Open Google Search Console and paste the affected URL into the URL Inspection tool at the top of the page. This shows you exactly what Google sees.</p>
<p>Check these details:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Coverage:</strong> Tells you the current status and any detected issues</li>
<li><strong>Sitemap:</strong> Shows whether Google found the URL through your sitemap</li>
<li><strong>Crawled page:</strong> Shows the HTML Googlebot received — compare this to what a visitor sees</li>
<li><strong>Indexing allowed:</strong> Confirms no noindex tag or robots.txt block</li>
</ul>
<p>If the "Crawled page" shows incomplete or broken HTML, your page may have JavaScript rendering issues that prevented Googlebot from seeing the full content.</p>
<h2>Step 3: Fix the Most Common Causes</h2>
<h3>Thin Content</h3>
<p>Google needs a reason to index your page. If the page has 200 words of generic text, Google skips it. Add genuine value: original research, step-by-step instructions, or data that is not available elsewhere.</p>
<h3>Internal Linking</h3>
<p>Pages with few or no internal links signal to Google that they are not important. Add contextual internal links from your most popular pages. Each link passes some authority and tells Google the page matters.</p>
<p>For more on structuring your site for better indexing, the <a href="https://www.praveentechworld.com/blog/technical-seo-checklist-for-beginners-fix-these-issues-to-rank-better">SEO checklist for beginners</a> covers the fundamentals.</p>
<h3>Duplicate Content</h3>
<p>If your page says the same thing as another page on your site, Google picks one version and excludes the rest. Use canonical tags to tell Google which version is the original. Consolidate similar pages into a single comprehensive resource.</p>
<h3>Crawl Budget Issues</h3>
<p>If your site has hundreds or thousands of pages, Google may not crawl all of them every time. Prioritize your most important pages in your sitemap and make sure your internal linking points to the pages that matter.</p>
<p>Google's crawl budget is limited. Do not waste it on low-value pages. The <a href="https://www.praveentechworld.com/blog/technical-seo-checklist-for-beginners-fix-these-issues-to-rank-better">technical SEO checklist</a> covers crawl budget and other technical fixes in more detail.</p>
<h2>Step 4: Request Indexing</h2>
<p>After making fixes, use the URL Inspection tool to request indexing. Click "Request Indexing" and Google puts the URL back in its crawl queue.</p>
<p>This is not instant. It can take a few days to a few weeks depending on your site's authority and how often Google crawls it. But submitting a manual request is faster than waiting for Google to rediscover the page.</p>
<h2>Step 5: Monitor the Index Coverage Report</h2>
<p>After requesting indexing for individual URLs, check the Index Coverage report in Search Console over the following weeks. Look for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Error count going down over time</li>
<li>Valid pages count going up</li>
<li>Specific error types that may need additional attention</li>
</ul>
<p>The report updates daily. If you see no change after two weeks, revisit the page content and internal links. Something is still blocking indexing.</p>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<p><strong>Q: How long does it take for Google to index a page after requesting indexing?</strong></p>
<p>A: Anywhere from a few days to a few weeks. New sites with low authority may take longer. If nothing changes after two weeks, the page likely has quality or technical issues that need fixing first.</p>
<p><strong>Q: Can I fix all index coverage errors at once?</strong></p>
<p>A: Some fixes apply site-wide (like improving overall content quality or fixing robots.txt), but each URL may have unique issues. Start with your most important pages and work through them individually.</p>
<p><strong>Q: Does submitting a sitemap fix indexing errors?</strong></p>
<p>A: A sitemap helps Google discover your URLs but does not guarantee indexing. Google still evaluates each page for quality and relevance before deciding whether to index it. A sitemap without good content will not solve the problem.</p>
<p><strong>Q: What is the difference between "Excluded" and "Error" in Index Coverage?</strong></p>
<p>A: "Excluded" means Google intentionally skipped the page (often correctly, like duplicate content). "Error" means Google tried but failed, usually due to technical issues. "Excluded" pages may be fine; "Error" pages need fixing.</p>
<p><strong>Q: Will deleting and recreating a page help with indexing?</strong></p>
<p>A: Rarely. Google remembers the URL. If the content was previously rejected, a new version with the same URL will likely face the same outcome. Improve the content at the existing URL instead of starting over.</p>
