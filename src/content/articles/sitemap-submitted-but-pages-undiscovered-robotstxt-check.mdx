## Direct Answer

Have you ever submitted a sitemap only to find that some pages remain undiscovered? You're not alone! Just because your sitemap shows as 'submitted successfully' doesn't guarantee that Google will index every page. Think of it more like a suggestion rather than a command. In my experience, the first things to look at are robots.txt, weak internal links, and canonical tags that might be misdirecting Google. If those look good, then it's worth checking page quality, redirects, noindex tags, and any server response issues.

## Explanation

This can be a bit confusing when you're navigating Google [Search Console](/articles/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes). You submit your XML sitemap, Google accepts it, and everything seems okay. But then you check the **Pages** report and find URLs marked as **Crawled - currently not indexed**, **Discovered - currently not indexed**, or **Excluded by 'noindex' tag**.

The main purpose of a sitemap is to inform Google about the existence of your URLs and how often they're updated. It doesn't force Google to crawl, index, or rank them. Google's own documentation explains this well: [Sitemaps overview](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/sitemaps/overview).

When a page stays undiscovered, it could be for a few common reasons. Google may find the URL in the sitemap but then hit a wall with your robots.txt file. If a page lacks internal links, Google might see it as isolated. There could also be a canonical tag pointing to another URL, which tells Google to ignore the page you want indexed.

This issue often pops up with newly published blog posts, product listings, category pages, and promotional landing pages. The sitemap may look fine, but if the page isn't well-linked from your homepage or related posts, or if it has a canonical tag that's just wrong, it can be problematic. So, I like to think of sitemap status as a starting point in troubleshooting, not the final answer.

## When This Fix Works

This fix is effective when Google can technically access the page but isn't crawling it due to mixed messages from your site. For instance, if your sitemap includes `/services/web-design/`, but your robots.txt file blocks `/services/`, Google sees the URL, checks the rules, and then decides to exclude it from crawling.

It can also help when pages lack internal links. If you've got a new page in your sitemap that isn't linked from key pages like your homepage or a related post, it may be sitting in a weak discovery state. From what I've seen, adding just a couple of contextual links from relevant pages can really help Google understand that this page is part of your site.

It's also relevant when canonical tags are incorrect. A common issue, especially with WordPress, is when a canonical tag points to the homepage or some unrelated page. If that's the case, Google might just follow that signal and ignore your desired page.

## When This Does NOT Work

However, this fix won't work if the page is considered thin, duplicated, or not valuable enough for Google to bother indexing. Simply having a sitemap won't make a low-value page worth crawling. Even if everything looks technically correct, Google might pass over a page with only a few words of duplicated content.

Also, if the URL returns an error (like 404, 410, 500, or even a soft 404), then your sitemap is pointing Google to a page that doesn't exist properly. You'll want to resolve the page's status first.

This approach won't fix ranking issues either. A page can be indexed but still rank poorly due to weak content or competition from stronger pages. For that, you'll need to focus on improving content and SEO, not just fixing the sitemap.

And if your website is brand new, it might just need some time. New sites often experience slower initial crawl rates from Google. I typically wait 2 to 6 weeks before making any judgments about crawl behavior, assuming the technical setup is correct.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Open Google [Search Console](/articles/how-to-enable-google-search-console-for-your-website) and select your website property from the menu on the left.

2. Click on **Sitemaps** under the **Indexing** section. Check the submitted sitemap URL, like `https://example.com/sitemap_index.xml`. If it's incorrect, remove it and submit the right one.

3. Open the **Pages** report under **Indexing**. Look for the affected URL in **Discovered - currently not indexed**, **Crawled - currently not indexed**, or **Not found (404)**. If the page isn't listed, paste the exact URL in the inspection field and hit Enter.

4. Run the URL Inspection test for the affected page. Check if Google says the URL is available. If it says it's blocked by robots.txt, you'll want to check that next.

5. Test robots.txt using Google's official tool. Head to the [robots.txt testing tool](https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/6062598) and enter your URL. If it's blocked, check your robots.txt file at `https://example.com/robots.txt`.

6. Remove any rules that block the page or section you want indexed. For example, change `Disallow: /blog/` to a more specific rule, or remove it altogether if you want all blog posts crawled.

7. Check the live page for a noindex tag. In your browser, right-click on the page and select **View Page Source**. Search for `noindex`. If you find `<meta name="robots" content="noindex">`, you'll need to remove that from your page settings or SEO plugin.

8. Check the canonical tag in the page source. Look for `canonical`. The URL inside the tag should match the page you want indexed. For instance, if your page is `https://example.com/services/web-design/`, the canonical should point to that same URL unless there's a specific reason to use a different one.

9. Fix incorrect canonicals in your CMS. If you're using WordPress, check the SEO plugin settings for the affected post or page. In Shopify, review the theme SEO settings. For custom sites, you may need to ask your developer to update the canonical template for that page type.

10. Add internal links from strong pages. Consider linking from your homepage, a related blog post, a service page, and a relevant category page. Use natural anchor text like "web design services" or "local SEO checklist."

11. If you have an HTML sitemap, add the page there as well. This is different from an XML sitemap and helps both visitors and search engines navigate through important sections.

12. After fixing the page, update your XML sitemap. With WordPress, you can use your SEO plugin's sitemap refresh option. If your sitemap is static, regenerate it and re-upload it.

13. Resubmit the sitemap in Google Search Console. Go to **Sitemaps**, enter the sitemap URL again, and click **Submit**. This notifies Google to check the latest version.

14. Use **Request Indexing** only after ensuring the page is clean. In the URL Inspection result, click **Request Indexing** only after you've addressed robots.txt, noindex, canonical, and internal link issues.

15. Wait and recheck the URL after 7 to 14 days. Open **URL Inspection** again. If Google still lists the page as not indexed, you may need to look at content quality, redirects, server logs, and duplicate pages.

## Alternatives / Related Fixes

1. Fix weak internal linking first. If a page lacks links from other pages, Google may see it as less important. Orphan pages are a common reason why a sitemap looks fine but pages remain undiscovered. Adding links from frequently crawled pages can help.

2. Correct duplicate or nearly duplicate pages. If you have `/service`, `/services`, and `/services/service`, Google might only choose one and ignore the others. Aim for a single clean URL, redirect the duplicates, and update your internal links accordingly.

3. Review crawl budget and site size. Smaller sites usually don't have crawl budget issues. However, larger sites with many low-value URLs can waste crawl activity on filter pages, tags, or outdated archives. It's important to block or noindex low-value sections only after understanding the potential impact.

4. Check server responses and redirects. If a page redirects from HTTP to HTTPS, non-www to www, or from an old slug to a new one, this can confuse your internal linking structure. Using a crawler or checking server logs can help confirm that the final URL returns a 200 OK status.

5. Reset or rebuild the sitemap as a last resort. If your sitemap plugin is acting up, showing deleted URLs, or mixing old URLs with new ones, consider rebuilding the sitemap and resubmitting it. I usually only do this after checking robots.txt, internal links, canonicals, noindex tags, and redirects, since a sitemap reset alone often doesn't resolve discovery issues.

If you need more help with setup, check out the [Website Setup & Indexing guide](/guides/website-setup) which covers sitemaps, WordPress configuration, 404 fixes, and canonical tags.

## Decision Summary

- If Google says the page is blocked by robots.txt, fix that issue before you touch the sitemap.
- If the page lacks internal links, add links from relevant pages that Google is already crawling.
- If the canonical tag points to a different URL, correct it to point to the page you want indexed.
- If the page has a noindex tag, remove that rule and then request indexing.
- If the page returns a 404, 410, 500, or soft 404, fix the status before resubmitting the sitemap.
- If the page is thin or duplicated, focus on improving the content or merging it with a stronger page instead of forcing indexing.

## FAQ

**Q: Why does Google Search Console say my sitemap was submitted successfully, but pages are still not indexed'**

**A:** Because sitemap submission only means Google received the file. It doesn't guarantee that Google will crawl or index every URL inside it. Google might skip pages due to robots.txt, noindex tags, weak internal links, canonical tags, redirects, server errors, or low content value.

**Q: Should I resubmit my sitemap every day until Google indexes my pages'**

**A:** No. Repeated resubmission usually doesn't help if there's a technical or quality issue. Instead, submit the sitemap after fixing any problems, then wait 7 to 14 days. If nothing changes, inspect the URL and check robots.txt, canonicals, internal links, and content quality.

**Q: Can robots.txt block a page even if it is in the sitemap'**

**A:** Yes. Google can discover a URL from the sitemap, check robots.txt, and decide not to crawl it. If you want the page crawled, you'll need to remove the blocking rule for that URL or section. You can test this using Google's robots.txt testing tool.

**Q: What is the difference between "Discovered - currently not indexed" and "Crawled - currently not indexed"'**

**A:** "Discovered - currently not indexed" means Google found the URL but hasn't crawled it yet. "Crawled - currently not indexed" means Google crawled the page but chose not to index it. The first often points to crawl priority, internal links, or sitemap timing. The second usually relates to content quality, duplication, or indexing rules.

**Q: What should I check first when sitemap pages stay undiscovered'**

**A:** Start by checking the URL in Google Search Console's **URL Inspection** tool. Then, look at robots.txt, noindex tags, canonical tags, redirects, and internal links. In my experience, those five checks solve most sitemap discovery problems before you need to rebuild the sitemap.
