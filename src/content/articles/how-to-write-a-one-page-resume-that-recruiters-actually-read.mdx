## Why This Matters

Think about it. You’ve got 7 seconds—that’s the average time a recruiter spends on your resume before deciding to keep it or toss it. If your resume is crammed into two pages of dense text, you’re basically hoping they’ll dig for the good stuff. They won’t. They’ve got 247 other resumes to get through.

A clean, one-page resume forces you to cut the noise. It puts your most impressive, relevant achievements right where they need to be. It shows you respect the reader’s time and can communicate your value clearly and quickly. In a world of short attention spans, that’s a massive advantage.

## What You Will Learn

*   **Which sections you absolutely must include** and which ones you can skip.
*   **How to write bullet points** that show impact, not just responsibility.
*   **The art of ruthless editing** to cut your content down by 40% or more.
*   **Formatting tricks** that create breathing room and make your resume scannable.
*   **How to tailor your content** for a specific job without writing a whole new document.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### 1. Start with a Blank Slate and Your Ego
Open a new document. Seriously. Don’t try to trim your old three-pager. That’s like trying to lose weight by wearing smaller pants. It doesn’t work. Instead, write down *only* your most recent 2-3 roles and your education. Start from scratch. This mental reset is key.

### 2. Adopt the "So What?" Test for Every Line
Go through each duty or responsibility you’ve written and ask yourself, "So what?" If you just list a task, the recruiter will ask the same thing. Turn duties into achievements with numbers. Did you "manage a team of 5"? So what. How about this: you "led a team of 5 to deliver a project 10% under budget." That’s the stuff that gets read. Aim for 3-5 powerful bullets per job.

### 3. Master the Minimalist Sections
Keep your contact info simple. Name, city, phone, email, LinkedIn. That’s it. Your summary should be 2-3 lines, not a whole paragraph. Use a "Skills" section, but only list 6-8 hard skills directly relevant to the job posting. And definitely ditch the "References available upon request" line—it’s assumed.

### 4. Perform the Ruthless Edit
Now, play editor. Can two bullets be combined? Is that first job from 10 years ago really relevant? If not, cut it. Does a skill seem obvious? Remove it. Your goal is to get the most impressive content onto one page. This usually means cutting 30-40% of what you first write. It’ll hurt, but it’s necessary.

### 5. Format for Scanability
Use clean, standard fonts like Calibri or Arial, size 10-11. Keep your margins between 0.5 and 1 inch. Use bold for your job titles and company names. Let white space be your friend. A dense wall of text is an instant eye-glazer. A well-formatted page should feel easy and inviting to read.

## Tips for Best Results

1.  **Kill your darlings.** You might love that sentence about your passion for project management. But if it doesn’t prove you’re good at it with a metric, it’s just fluff. Cut it.
2.  **One page means one page.** Don’t cheat with tiny fonts or 0.2 margins. If it spills over by even three words, go back and edit. Recruiters notice this stuff.
3.  **Lead with your strengths.** Put your most recent and impressive role at the top. If you have a great degree, put education high. Forgo the "Objective" and go with a "Summary of Qualifications."
4.  **Use keywords from the job description.** Look at the top 5 required skills in the posting. Make sure those exact words (e.g., "data analysis," "client onboarding") appear in your resume. This helps get past automated filters.
5.  **Save as a PDF.** Always. Unless they specifically ask for a Word doc. This locks in your formatting so it looks the same on their screen as it does on yours.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What if I have 10+ years of experience?**
Focus on the last 10-15 years. Anything older can be summarized as "Earlier Career: [Role] at [Company]" or listed in a "Selected Earlier Experience" section with just 1-2 lines. You don’t need to detail jobs from 1999.

**How do I handle a career gap?**
Address it head-on, briefly. In your work history, you can have a line like: "2022-2023: Career break for professional development/caregiving." Then, in your summary, you can mention any relevant skills you gained during that time (like freelance work, courses, or certifications).

**I don’t have much experience. How do I fill a page?**
Focus on what you do have: projects, internships, volunteer work, and relevant coursework. Create sections like "Academic Projects" or "Volunteer Experience" and use the same achievement-based bullet points. Highlight your education, including your GPA if it’s good, and any relevant coursework.

## Conclusion

A one-page resume isn’t about hiding your experience. It’s about showcasing it. You’re forced to tell a tight, compelling story of your value. In those 7 seconds, you want the recruiter to see your best wins, not wade through a novel. Edit hard, format clean, and prove your impact. That’s how you get the interview.
