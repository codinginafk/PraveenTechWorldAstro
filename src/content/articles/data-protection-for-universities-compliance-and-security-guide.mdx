## Why Data Protection Matters More Than Ever for Universities

Have you ever thought about how much sensitive information a university manages? It’s actually a staggering amount—often more than even some government agencies handle! From student records to financial aid details, medical histories, and research data, there's a ton of personal information stored in one place. Just imagine the impact of a single data breach; it could expose thousands of records and lead to hefty fines, lawsuits, and a serious loss of trust.

In fact, in 2025, higher education institutions became the third most targeted sector for ransomware attacks, according to Check Point Research. Why? Well, universities are a treasure trove of personal data, they often rely on outdated IT systems, and they have to juggle security with the need for openness and collaboration. This means that data protection is everyone's job on campus—not just the IT folks.

## The Rules: FERPA, GDPR, and CCPA

### FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act)

FERPA, or the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act, is a U.S. law designed to protect student education records. It gives students rights to access their records, request changes, and control who sees their private info. Although this law has been around since 1974, it sometimes struggles to keep up with today’s tech landscape. Here are some key things to remember:

- **What counts as an education record:** Basically, it covers almost any record about a student that the school keeps, including emails, grade sheets, advising notes, and disciplinary records.
- **Outside vendors:** If schools are using cloud services for things like learning tools or analytics, they need to have data-sharing agreements in place with those vendors to comply with FERPA.
- **Directory information:** Schools can share basic info like names, addresses, phone numbers, and dates attended without permission, but they must allow students to opt out of this.

### GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

If your university is processing data of EU residents, you need to be aware of GDPR. This could apply to study abroad programs, online courses with EU students, or collaborations with European partners. Here’s what GDPR requires:

- A valid reason for processing personal data.
- Conducting data protection checks for high-risk processing.
- Notifying individuals about breaches within 72 hours.
- Appointing a Data Protection Officer (DPO).
- Respecting the right to be forgotten, meaning individuals can request their data to be erased.

### CCPA/CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act)

In California, the CCPA kicks in when a university collects personal information from residents. Public schools have some exemptions, but private schools or those with California students need to comply. The CPRA, which became effective in 2023, added new rules regarding sensitive data and strengthened enforcement.

## Common Security Risks in Higher Education

### 1. Phishing and Social Engineering

University email addresses are often easy to guess and publicly available, making them prime targets for cybercriminals. Staff and students receive phishing attempts aimed at stealing passwords, making unauthorized transfers, and accessing sensitive information. According to the 2025 Verizon Data Breach Report, over 60% of breaches in the education sector were related to phishing attacks.
