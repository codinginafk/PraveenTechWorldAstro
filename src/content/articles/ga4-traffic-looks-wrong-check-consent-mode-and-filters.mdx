## A Common Dilemma

Have you ever looked at your GA4 traffic numbers and thought, "This can't be right-- You're not alone! Many folks face this issue after setting up their website. But don't worry, I'm here to help you figure it out. Let's explore some easy settings to check first: Consent Mode, GA4 data filters, and cross-domain tracking.

## Here's What I Discovered

From my experience, most of the time when people see "bad GA4 data," it's not just random. It usually comes down to a few key things, like a cookie banner blocking analytics, filters that might be excluding real users, or issues with tracking across different domains. Before you go reinstalling your analytics plugin or your Tag Manager, let's troubleshoot these settings!

GA4 is quite different from basic page load counters. It tracks users, sessions, events, consent states, and more. So, any changes made after you set everything up can cause your reports to show unexpected drops or splits in data.

### Start with Consent Mode

Consent Mode is usually the first place I look. If your cookie banner sets `analytics_storage` to denied before the GA4 tag has a chance to load, it could lead to incomplete data. I've seen this happen firsthand; one client saw a 36% drop in traffic right after a new cookie banner went live. The reason? Many visitors simply didn't click **Accept** for analytics cookies.

### Check Your Data Filters

Misconfigured filters can also cause trouble. If your filters are too broad, they might accidentally exclude legitimate traffic--like visitors from your internal team or developers. I recommend diving into Google's [GA4 data filters documentation](https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/11593727) for more insights. I've encountered cases where an office IP was added correctly, but the filter ended up being too broad, removing visitors from the same network.

### Cross-Domain Tracking Challenges

Cross-domain tracking can be tricky too. If it's not set up right, GA4 might mistake one visitor as two separate users--one on your main site and another when they reach your checkout or payment pages. If the GA4 linker doesn't capture the client ID, it thinks it's a new session. I've seen websites lose significant conversions because of this.

Also, keep in mind that GA4 may take a day or two to settle down after you make changes. If you adjust your Consent Mode at 2 p.m. and check reports an hour later, you might still see a mix of old and new data.

It's worth noting that GA4 users and Google [Search Console](/articles/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes) clicks are different metrics. They provide valuable insights but capture different aspects of user behavior. If you want to dive deeper into this, check out my post on [Google Search Console Not Showing Data](/blog/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes).

## When This Fix Works

You'll find this fix helpful if your GA4 traffic changed right after setting up your website, launching a cookie banner, updating Tag Manager, or adding a new domain. It's also useful when traffic looks fine in Realtime but seems low in standard reports.

If you can replicate the problem, that's great! For example, try opening your site in a private window: decline cookies, then accept them, and check what GA4 records when moving to the checkout domain.

## When This Doesn't Work

Just a heads-up: these solutions won't change historical GA4 data. Filters only impact reports after they're implemented, and changes to Consent Mode won't alter past data either.

Also, GA4 won't match other [analytics tools](/articles/google-analytics-4-page-tracking-how-it-works) exactly. Different tools like GA4 and server logs have their own rules, so I see server logs as a useful check, not an exact match.

And remember, this doesn't solve every privacy issue. Some visitors block scripts or use private browsing, which means you can't force every browser to send the same data.

Lastly, if you're facing attribution problems, GA4 may categorize traffic differently. In that case, you'll need to check your campaign tags and referrer settings too.

## Step-by-Step Breakdown

1. **Check Your GA4 Data Stream ID**

   Open GA4 and click **Admin** in the lower left. Under **Data collection and modification**, select **Data streams**. Open your web stream and copy the **[Measurement ID](/articles/ga4-data-delayed-or-missing-check-measurement-id-consent)** (it looks like `G-XXXXXXXXXX`).

   Next, check your website source, Tag Manager container, or analytics plugin to ensure the same `G-` ID is used everywhere. If you find discrepancies, choose one as the main property and remove the duplicate.

2. **Verify GA4 Tag Firing in Google Tag Manager**

   Open Google Tag Manager and click **Preview** in the upper right. Enter your website URL and click **Connect**. 

   Navigate through your home page, a service page, and the main conversion page. On the left, check **Tags Fired**. Your GA4 Configuration tag should fire on **All Pages**. If it doesn't, fix that before diving into filters or cross-domain settings.

3. **Check Consent Mode Before the GA4 Tag Loads**

   Look at your cookie banner settings. Ensure it controls `analytics_storage`. If you're running ads in the EEA, also check `ad_storage`, `ad_user_data`, and `ad_personalization`.

   In GTM, check **Consent Overview**. You want to see a default consent state before the page loads. If the GA4 tag fires before the cookie consent is set, your reports might look off.

4. **Test Accepted and Declined Consent States**

   Open your site in a private window and decline analytics cookies, then reload the page. Check the consent state for `analytics_storage` in GTM.

   Now, accept cookies and reload the page again. You should see a clear change from denied to granted. If it doesn't change, your cookie banner might not be sending the right signals to GTM.

5. **Review GA4 Data Filters**

   Go back to GA4 and click **Admin**. Under **Data collection and modification**, select **Data settings** and then **Data filters**. 

   Check the status of each filter. If you see an internal traffic filter, check the IP rule. Avoid using broad CIDR ranges unless you're certain they only cover your office or VPN.

6. **Examine the `traffic_type` Parameter**

   If you're using GTM, check your GA4 Configuration tag for the `traffic_type` parameter. If it's used incorrectly, it can break reports. Remove any bad `traffic_type` values unless you have a solid reason to keep them.

7. **Inspect Internal IP Filtering**

   Visit a site like whatismyipaddress.com to find your public IP address. Then, check your internal traffic filter in GA4 to confirm the IP rule.

   If you work from home, be cautious about adding single home IPs as they can change often. Instead, use a stable office VPN IP range.

8. **Set Up Cross-Domain Tracking**

   In GA4, go to **Admin** and click on **Data streams**. Open your web stream and scroll to **Configure tag settings**. Add each domain where users continue their journey, like `example.com`, `shop.example.net`, etc.

   Only include domains that are part of your owned customer journey.

9. **Test the Cross-Domain Handoff**

   Open your main domain in a private window and click the link that directs users to the checkout domain. Check the destination URL for a `_gl` parameter. 

   Then, look at the `_ga` cookie in your browser's DevTools before and after the domain switch. If the client ID changes, there's still an issue with cross-domain tracking.

10. **Check Realtime and DebugView**

    Go to **Reports** in GA4, then **Realtime**. Open your site in a private window, accept analytics cookies, and see if your visit appears in Realtime.

    Next, head to **Admin**, click on **DebugView**, and repeat the test. You should see one user flow, not two separate users.

11. **Review Main Reports After 24-48 Hours**

    After making fixes, wait at least 24 hours. GA4 standard reports can lag behind Realtime. Compare the same date range before and after the fix.

## Alternatives / Related Fixes

1. **Fix the Cookie Banner Order**

   If Consent Mode looks off, the issue might be with the order of scripts. The CMP should set default consent before the GA4 tag fires.

2. **Check WordPress Caching and Plugin Conflicts**

   If you're using WordPress, be sure to clear the site cache after changing analytics tags. Also, check for multiple analytics plugins that might create conflicts.

3. **Fix Duplicated Tags**

   Use GTM Preview to see if the GA4 tag fires once per page. If it fires multiple times, it can distort your data. Ensure you have only one clean source of truth for GA4.

4. **Review Campaign Tags and Referrers**

   If traffic looks off in source and medium reports, check your UTM tags for accuracy. Consistency is key here!

5. **Reinstall or Reset Only as a Last Resort**

   I usually see reinstalling or resetting as a last option. It can mess up working settings and create duplicates if you forget to remove old code.

## Let's Wrap It Up

If your traffic dropped after a cookie banner launch, start with Consent Mode. If Realtime looks fine but standard reports seem low, wait 24-48 hours before checking filters and attribution. 

If users are missing from your office or VPN visits, double-check **Data filters** and internal IP rules. And if your users split between domains, focus on fixing cross-domain tracking.
