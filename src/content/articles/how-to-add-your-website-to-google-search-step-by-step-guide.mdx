So, you've just built your website, and it looks fantastic! But when you search for `site:yourdomain.com` on Google, nothing shows up. Frustrating, right?
Don't worry; this is totally normal! Google just doesn't know about your site yet, and you'll need to give it a little nudge. Thankfully, this process is pretty straightforward and should take around 15 minutes (or maybe a bit longer if you're new to it). If you're still building your site, you might want to check out our [complete [beginner guide](/articles/how-to-build-a-website-from-scratch-in-2026-a-complete-beginner-guide) to building a website] first.(/blog/how-to-build-a-website-from-scratch-in-2026-a-complete-beginner-guide).

Let me share the exact steps I followed to get praveentechworld.com indexed in under 24 hours. Here's how you can do it too!

## Method 1: Google [Search Console](/articles/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes) (Required)

The first thing you'll need is Google Search Console (GSC). It's the official way to let Google know that your site exists. If you haven't set it up yet, take a quick detour to read our [full GSC setup guide](/blog/how-to-set-up-google-search-console-for-your-new-website). It covers everything from verification to property types and the initial setup.

Once you're verified, you can submit your URL directly:

1. Open the URL Inspection tool in Search Console.
2. Paste your homepage URL.
3. Click "Request Indexing."

Google usually crawls your URL within minutes and adds it to the index. This method is great for individual pages, but it might not be the fastest way to index your entire site.

## Method 2: Submit Your Sitemap

Next up is submitting your sitemap. A sitemap is a file that lists all the pages on your site that you want Google to index. Most modern site builders automatically generate one for you.

To find your sitemap, just go to `yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml`. If you see a bunch of XML content with URLs, you're all set! If you get a 404 error or something similar, check out our guide on [fixing sitemap errors](/blog/how-to-fix-sitemap-errors-in-google-search-console).

To submit it in Search Console:

1. Go to the Sitemaps section in the left menu.
2. Paste `sitemap.xml` in the field.
3. Click Submit.

Google checks sitemaps regularly and crawls all the listed URLs, making this the most effective way to add your entire website to Google Search at once.

## Method 3: Use IndexNow (Speed Boost)

Now, here's a little trick: IndexNow. This tool helps search engines crawl your URLs right away. While Google doesn't officially support it, using it can speed things up since faster indexing on Bing often translates to quicker indexing on Google as well.

Most hosting platforms and CMS tools have IndexNow built-in. If yours does, enable it in your settings. You'll just be sending a quick notification to Bing's IndexNow service with your URLs.

For the best results, try using IndexNow alongside sitemap submission. They work well together!

## Method 4: Let Google Find You Naturally

Did you know that Google is always on the lookout for new content? If other sites link to yours, Google will eventually discover those links and find your site.

Forget the old "Submit URL to Google" page; that feature was discontinued in 2020. Instead, focus on:

- Publishing content that naturally attracts backlinks.
- Sharing your articles on social platforms like LinkedIn and X.
- Syndicating your content to high-authority sites like Dev.to or Medium (they get crawled pretty quickly).
- Keeping your sitemap updated as you add new pages.

## What Not to Do

You might come across "SEO experts" suggesting you submit your URL to hundreds of search engines. Please don't do this! Google, Bing, and Yahoo cover over 95% of search traffic. The rest is just noise.

Also, steer clear of "guaranteed indexing" services; they often turn out to be scams. Indexing depends on your content quality, site structure, and server availability--there's no way to skip these.

And definitely avoid black-hat techniques like cloaking or doorway pages. Google doesn't take kindly to these tactics, and recovering from penalties can take months.

## Troubleshooting: Google Found My Site But Nothing Shows Up

If your site is indexed but still doesn't appear in search results, there might be another issue. Check out our guide on [Google Search Console showing no data](/blog/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes) for specific fixes.

Some common reasons Google might find your site but ignore it include:

- A recent domain name with no trust signals.
- Thin content (less than 300 words per page).
- Duplicate content from another indexed site.
- Robots.txt blocking Googlebot.
- A noindex tag on your pages.

Make sure to look into these before assuming the indexing process has failed.

## How Long Does It Take?
With sitemap submission and URL inspection, you can expect initial indexing within 24 to 72 hours. Full content discovery might take 1 to 4 weeks, depending on your site's authority and how often you publish.

In my case, I had 25 indexed pages in the first month using these methods. As of June 2026, Google is even showing impressions for searches like "add website to google search" and "[google analytics](/articles/google-analytics-for-beginners-how-to-track-your-website-traffic) page tracking," even though my site is just a month old.

The key is consistency. Submit your site once, keep publishing content regularly, and give Google's crawlers time to do their thing. If your sitemap is updated and your content stays fresh, indexing should take care of itself.

## What to Do If Nothing Works

Sometimes, despite following all the steps, Google might still ignore your site. Here's what that might mean:

**Your domain is too new.** Google is cautious with recently registered domains. If your domain is brand new, it might take time for Google to trust it. There's not much you can do but keep publishing quality content and allow your domain to age.

**Your content is too thin.** If your pages are filled with generic text, Google might not see a reason to index or rank them. Aim for unique content: original research, personal experiences, or in-depth guides.

**Your site has technical problems.** If Googlebot can't reach your server or your pages are throwing errors, Google won't stick around. Running a site health check using tools like Screaming Frog or Sitebulb can help identify these issues.

**Your site is blocked by manual action.** Check Google Search Console for any Manual Actions listed under Security & Manual Actions. If you see one, it means your site has violated Google's spam policies, and you'll need to fix the issue before submitting a reconsideration request.

In all these cases, the best approach is to address what you can (like content quality or technical issues) and then wait for the rest. As your site proves to be reliable over time, Google's crawlers will visit more frequently.

## Tracking Your Progress

After you've submitted everything, keep an eye on your indexing progress weekly:

- Check the Index Coverage report in Search Console.
- Search `site:yourdomain.com` and count the indexed pages.
- Monitor the Sitemaps report for any errors or warnings.
- Use the URL Inspection tool for individual pages.

I kept track of these numbers every Monday for the first couple of months. It was motivating to see the indexed page count grow week after week, even when search traffic was a bit slow at first. The indexing came first, and then the traffic followed.
