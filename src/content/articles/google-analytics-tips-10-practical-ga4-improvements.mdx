After using GA4 for over a year across multiple projects, I picked up a set of configuration changes that made the biggest difference to data quality. Most of these took under 10 minutes to implement. Here they are in order of impact.

## 1. Turn On Enhanced Measurement

This is the easiest win. Enhanced measurement automatically tracks page views, scrolls, outbound clicks, site search, video engagement, and file downloads. No code needed.

Check it under Admin > Data Streams > your web stream > Enhanced Measurement. Make sure all toggles are on.

If you skipped this during setup, you have been missing data on scroll depth and outbound clicks without knowing it. Turn it on now and the data starts flowing immediately.

## 2. Exclude Internal Traffic

Your own visits pollute your analytics. Every time you test a page or preview a change, GA4 counts it as a real visit. Over weeks, this adds up and makes your engagement metrics look worse than they are.

Create an internal traffic filter in GA4:
- Go to Admin > Data Settings > Data Filters
- Create a new filter with type "Internal Traffic"
- Add your IP address as the parameter
- Set it to "Testing" first, then switch to "Active" once you confirm it works

Do not skip the testing phase. I once filtered out half my traffic because my IP address had changed and I forgot to update it.

## 3. Mark Key Events

GA4 lets you mark certain events as conversions. These appear in a dedicated report and are easier to track over time.

For a content site, mark these as key events:
- First scroll (people read past the fold)
- Video start (if you embed videos)
- Form submission (if you have a contact or signup form)

Marking too many events as conversions defeats the purpose. Pick 3 to 5 that directly relate to your goals.

If you need help setting up proper event tracking, the [GA4 page tracking guide](/blog/google-analytics-4-page-tracking-how-it-works) explains how page events work.

## 4. Add Exclusions for Bot Traffic

GA4 filters known bots automatically, but not all of them. If you see traffic from places like data centers or known proxy IPs, exclude them.

Go to Admin > Data Streams > your web stream > Define Internal Traffic and add IP ranges you want to exclude. Common ones include AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure IP ranges.

If your analytics shows traffic from places like Iowa or South Carolina where your audience is not based, bot traffic is likely inflating your numbers.

## 5. Create a Custom Dashboard

The default GA4 reporting interface is not great. Customize it by creating explorations that show exactly what you need.

Start with a free-form exploration that shows:
- Page title and page location as rows
- Views, engagement rate, and average engagement time as metrics
- Add a filter for the last 28 days

Save this as your default view. It beats navigating through the standard reports every time.

## 6. Set Up Monthly Email Reports

GA4 can email you automated reports. This is useful if you want a regular check-in without logging in.

Go to Admin > Property Settings > Reporting Identity and configure your email preferences. Set up a monthly summary that goes to your inbox.

I read mine on the first of every month. If something looks off, I investigate. If everything looks normal, I skip the deep dive and save an hour.

## 7. Use URL Parameters Correctly

If you add tracking parameters (UTM codes) to your links, be consistent. GA4 groups traffic by source, medium, and campaign. If you use "social" as the medium in one link and "social-media" in another, GA4 treats them as separate channels.

Standardize your UTM values:
- Source: google, twitter, linkedin, newsletter
- Medium: organic, social, email, referral
- Campaign: use descriptive names like "summer-promo" or "weekly-digest"

If your acquisition reports look messy, inconsistent UTM codes are probably the reason. The [GA4 content strategy guide](/blog/how-to-use-google-analytics-4-to-improve-your-content-strategy) covers how to interpret acquisition data.

## 8. Delete Unused Events

Over time, GA4 accumulates events from old experiments, deleted features, and temporary tracking. These clutter your reports and make it harder to find what matters.

Go to Admin > Data Display > Events and review your event list. Delete any that are no longer active. GA4 keeps the historical data, so you do not lose anything.

I found 12 unused events on my site when I first checked. Cleaning them made my reports significantly clearer.

## 9. Set Correct Time Zone

This sounds obvious but many people get it wrong. GA4 uses the time zone you set during property creation. If you set it to the wrong time zone, your daily reports will be off.

Check it under Admin > Property Settings > Reporting Time Zone. Changing it affects future data only. Historical data stays in the old time zone.

If your audience is in a different time zone from yours, set GA4 to their time zone for more accurate daily reporting.

## 10. Link GA4 to Google Search Console

This connection shows you which search queries bring traffic and how those pages perform in terms of engagement.

Go to Admin > Property Settings > Search Console Links and add your site. The data appears in GA4's acquisition reports under "Google Organic Search Traffic."

This is the most actionable connection you can make. It tells you exactly what people search for before landing on your site. If your content engagement guide is getting traffic for the wrong keywords, you will see it here.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long do GA4 changes take to show effect?**
A: Most configuration changes take effect within a few hours. Data filters and event changes may take up to 24 hours to process. If you change something and see no difference after 48 hours, double-check the configuration.

**Q: Can I undo a data filter?**
A: Yes, but filtered data is permanently removed from your reports after the filter becomes active. Always test filters first and keep a backup of your raw data if possible.

**Q: Do these tips work for any type of website?**
A: Most of them do. Enhanced measurement, internal traffic exclusion, and key events are useful for almost every site. UTM standardization matters most if you do marketing campaigns. Time zone settings matter for daily reporting.

**Q: How often should I review my GA4 setup?**
A: Every three months. Settings drift over time as Google updates GA4 and as you make changes to your site. A quarterly review catches issues before they affect months of data.
