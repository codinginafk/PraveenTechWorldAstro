## How to Ask for a Raise. Email Template Included.

I got a 15% raise using exactly this template. Here it is. Asking for more money is terrifying. It feels like you're begging, and rejection is scary. But here's the truth: companies expect you to ask. They budget for raises. If you don't ask, you might get a tiny 2% cost-of-living adjustment instead of what you're truly worth.

This isn't about being greedy. It's about being professional and aligning your salary with the value you deliver every single day. This guide will remove the fear and give you the exact words to use.

## Why This Matters (The Real Problem This Solves)

You're probably leaving money on the table right now. The gap between your initial offer and your market value grows with every successful project you complete. If you don't close that gap, you're working for less than you deserve.

Not negotiating can cost you tens of thousands of dollars over your career. A $5,000 raise now is massive when you factor in future raises based on that higher salary. This matters for your financial health and your career confidence.

## What You Will Learn

*   How to pick the perfect time to ask, so you’re not just another request on a busy manager's list.
*   The simple method to calculate and prove your value with hard data, not just feelings.
*   A word-for-word email template you can adapt in 10 minutes.
*   What to say and do during the actual conversation to stay calm and convincing.
*   How to handle any response, from "yes" to "not right now," like a pro.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### 1. Time Your Ask Perfectly

Don't ask during a crisis or on a random Tuesday. The best windows are right after a major win—like launching a big project or saving a key client—or during performance review cycles. Your manager is already in a mindset to evaluate your contribution. Another good time is when you've taken on significant new responsibilities that aren't in your original job description.

### 2. Build Your "Brag Sheet"

This is your evidence file. Don't just say "I work hard." Prove it. Open a doc and list:
*   **Specific accomplishments:** "Increased client retention by 12% in Q3 through a new follow-up process."
*   **Added responsibilities:** "Now also manage the internship program, which took 5 hours a week off the team lead's plate."
*   **Positive feedback:** Screenshot that email from a happy client or colleague.
*   **Market data:** Use sites like Glassdoor or Levels.fyi to find the average salary for your role in your city with your experience.

### 3. Craft the Email (Use This Template!)

Send this email to schedule a meeting. Never ask for a raise via email itself.

**Subject:** Meeting to discuss my role and compensation

**Hi [Manager's Name],**

**I'd like to schedule some time to meet next week to discuss my current role, my recent contributions, and my compensation.**

**I'm really proud of what I've accomplished over the past year, including [mention 1-2 huge wins, like "leading the X project to a successful launch" or "helping us achieve Y metric"]. I'm excited about the direction of the team and want to ensure my compensation reflects the value I'm delivering.**

**Do you have 20 minutes available on [suggest a day/time]?**

**Thanks,**
**[Your Name]**

### 4. The Conversation: Your Script

When you meet, be confident. Start with your prepared points. "Thanks for meeting. As I mentioned, I've been focused on delivering results like [briefly state your top win]. Based on my expanded responsibilities and the market data I've reviewed, I'm requesting an adjustment to my salary to [$ your desired amount or range]."

Then, be quiet. Let them respond. Don't fill the silence. It’s their turn to talk.

## Tips for Best Results

1.  **Practice out loud.** Seriously, say your pitch to a friend or in the shower. It builds muscle memory and calms nerves.
2.  **Focus on value, not need.** "I need more money because rent went up" is a weak argument. "My work has increased team efficiency by 20%, justifying an investment in my role" is strong.
3.  **Get the first number out.** Aim high but reasonable. If you say "$70,000 to $75,000," they'll likely counter near the bottom of that range. If you say nothing, they'll anchor to your current salary.
4.  **It's a negotiation.** Be prepared for them to meet you in the middle. Your "walk-away" number is the lowest you'll accept.
5.  **Get it in writing.** If they say yes, follow up with a polite email summarizing the agreement and the effective date. "Thanks for the great conversation! Just to confirm, my new salary of $X will be effective starting on Y date."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What if my boss says "no, not right now"?**
A: Stay calm. Don't get defensive. Ask, "Thank you for letting me know. Could we set some clear, measurable goals for me to achieve over the next 6 months so we can revisit this then?" This turns a "no" into a "later" with a plan.

**Q: How big of a raise should I ask for?**
A: A 10-15% increase is a standard, reasonable ask for a significant promotion or pay adjustment. If you're severely underpaid, you can ask for more, but back it up with solid market data. A 3-5% raise is often just a cost-of-living adjustment, not a true performance raise.

**Q: Should I mention I have another offer?**
A: Only if you do and you're willing to take it. Using a real offer as leverage can work, but it's risky. It can make your current employer feel you're already out the door. It's better to focus the conversation on your value within their company.

## Conclusion

Asking for a raise is a skill. Like any skill, it gets easier with practice. The worst they can say is no, and even then, you've started a dialogue about your worth. You've planted a seed. That 15% raise I got? It changed my finances completely. You owe it to yourself to have the conversation. Prepare your case, send the email, and go get what you've earned.
