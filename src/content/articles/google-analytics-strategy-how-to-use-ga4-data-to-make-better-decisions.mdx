Most people set up Google Analytics and immediately start looking at page views. I did the same thing. It felt productive. I could see numbers going up and down and pretend I understood what was happening.

The truth is that page views, sessions, and users are vanity metrics for most sites. They tell you how much traffic you got but not whether that traffic is doing anything useful. A real analytics strategy starts with a different question: what decision am I trying to make?

## Step 1: Define What Success Looks Like

Before you look at any numbers, write down what a successful visit looks like for each page type on your site.

For a blog post, success might be:
- Reading past the first scroll (engagement rate above 50%)
- Clicking an internal link to another article
- Subscribing to a newsletter

For a product page, success might be:
- Adding an item to the cart
- Reaching the checkout page
- Completing a purchase

The metrics you track should come from these goals, not the other way around. If you do not know what success looks like, every number in GA4 is equally meaningless.

GA4 has a concept called events that makes this easier. Instead of tracking generic page views, you track specific actions people take. If you are new to GA4 events, the [GA4 page tracking guide](/blog/google-analytics-4-page-tracking-how-it-works) covers the basics.

## Step 2: Set Up Meaningful Events

GA4 automatically tracks a few events like page views, scrolls, and outbound clicks. These are a good starting point. But the real value comes from custom events tied to your specific goals.

For my site, I created events for:
- **Article read** — fires when someone scrolls past 75% of an article
- **Tool used** — tracks when someone opens a free tool I built
- **Newsletter signup** — tracks form submissions on the email list

Each event has parameters that add context. The article read event includes the article title and category. The newsletter event includes the signup source.

Setting up custom events in GA4 requires minor code changes but is worth the effort. Without them, you are guessing what people do on your site.

For a full walkthrough of GA4 tracking, see the [content engagement guide](/blog/how-to-track-content-engagement-in-google-analytics-4).

## Step 3: Focus on Engagement Rate, Not Bounce Rate

GA4 replaced bounce rate with engagement rate for a reason. Bounce rate penalizes pages where people found what they needed and left satisfied. Engagement rate rewards pages where people interacted with the content.

This is a fundamental shift. In Universal Analytics, a high bounce rate was always bad. In GA4, a page with 60% engagement rate might be performing well if people read the content and got their answer.

I stopped worrying about bounce rate entirely after switching to GA4. What matters is whether people who visit my content actually engage with it and whether they come back. Both are visible in GA4's engagement reports.

## Step 4: Use Acquisition Reports to Find What Works

GA4's acquisition reports show where your traffic comes from: organic search, social media, direct visits, referrals, and email. But the raw numbers are not the full story.

What matters is acquisition quality. A channel that brings 100 visitors with 80% engagement rate is better than a channel that brings 1000 visitors with 10% engagement rate. The first channel actually delivers value.

To find this, look at each channel's engagement rate and average engagement time. If social media brings traffic but people leave within 10 seconds, that traffic is not useful. You may need to change your social strategy or accept that social is a discovery tool rather than a traffic driver.

## Step 5: Create a Reporting Cadence

Data is useless if you never look at it. Set a regular schedule for reviewing your analytics.

I check three things every week:
- Top pages by engagement rate (not page views)
- Channels with the highest quality traffic
- Any sudden drops or spikes that need investigation

Once a month, I do a deeper review of content performance and compare it to the previous month. This is when I decide what content to update, what to retire, and what new topics to write about.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the most important metric in GA4?**
A: It depends on your goals. For content sites, engagement rate and returning users are usually more useful than page views or sessions. For ecommerce, conversion rate and average order value matter most. Always start with your goals, not the available metrics.

**Q: How many events should I track in GA4?**
A: Start with 3 to 5 custom events tied to your core goals. Adding too many events creates noise and makes it harder to spot meaningful trends. You can always add more later as you understand what matters.

**Q: Should I still use Universal Analytics data?**
A: UA stopped processing new data. Use it as a reference for historical trends but build your current strategy on GA4 data. The two systems measure things differently, so direct comparisons can be misleading.

**Q: How do I know if my GA4 strategy is working?**
A: You should be able to answer three questions: (1) Where is my best traffic coming from? (2) What content drives the most engagement? (3) What is the one change I should make based on last month's data? If you cannot answer these, your strategy needs work.

**Q: Can I automate GA4 reporting?**
A: Yes. GA4 has an API and built-in scheduled email reports. You can also connect GA4 to Google Looker Studio for custom dashboards. But automated reports should complement manual analysis, not replace it.
