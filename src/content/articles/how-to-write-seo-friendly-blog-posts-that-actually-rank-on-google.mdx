## How to Write SEO Friendly Blog Posts That Actually Rank on Google

Have you ever poured your heart and soul into writing what you believe is the perfect article? You've researched, crafted sentences, and even double-checked your facts. But after hitting publish and sharing it on social media, the response is... crickets? It can be so disheartening! The reality is that there's a vast ocean of content out there, and even the most brilliant piece can get lost on page 50 of Google's search results. 

This isn't just about playing games with algorithms; it's about communicating effectively with both search engines and the readers you want to help. The difference between a post that's ignored and one that draws in consistent traffic lies in how you structure it and the strategies you employ. If you're curious about the technical side of things, check out our [technical SEO checklist](/blog/technical-seo-checklist-for-beginners-fix-these-issues-to-rank-better) for some behind-the-scenes tips. But let's dive into the strategy itself, step-by-step, so your next post stands a real chance of ranking.

### Start with Keyword Research

Before you even begin to write, the key to a successful post starts with thorough keyword research. And trust me, this is not about cramming a keyword into your content five times! It's about grasping the exact challenges your potential readers are facing. For instance, if someone types "how to fix a slow computer" into Google, they might be looking for simple, clear steps rather than a technical deep dive. They likely just want a quick, safe solution they can follow right away. Your job is to identify and address those specific needs.

A good way to kick things off is by using [free tools](/articles/how-to-automate-your-daily-workflow-with-free-tools-in-2026-complete-guide) like Google's Keyword Planner or Ubersuggest. Start with a general topic related to what you know, like "home office setup." Instead of just chasing the highest volume keyword, dig into "long-tail" keywords - those longer, more specific phrases with less competition. You might discover phrases like "ergonomic home office setup for back pain" or "small apartment home office ideas under $500." These can be goldmines because they reveal exactly what searchers are looking for. A good tip is to find keywords that have decent monthly search volume but low-to-medium difficulty. If you notice smaller blogs or forums ranking for these terms, that's a sign you might have a chance to compete!

### Build Comprehensive Content

Once you have your primary keyword, it's time to create a thorough piece that answers all related questions. This is where the idea of topical authority becomes important. Google loves content that shows a deep understanding of the subject. For example, if your main keyword is "starting a vegetable garden," your article should cover related areas like selecting the right seeds, soil preparation, essential tools, and common mistakes beginners make. You can check the "People Also Ask" boxes in [Google search](/articles/how-to-add-your-website-to-google-search-step-by-step-guide) results to uncover related queries. Scrolling to the bottom of the search results page might also reveal additional questions you can address. By the end, your post should be the go-to resource on that topic - not just a quick fix.

### Write with a Plan

Now that you've done your research, it's time to start writing, but don't forget to have a clear structure in mind. Your primary keyword (and its variations) should be naturally woven into specific parts of your post. First off, make sure it's in your title tag. This is the clickable headline users see in search results, and it should be compelling and promise a solution. For example, "The Beginner's Guide to Starting a Vegetable Garden in 2026" is much better than simply "Gardening Tips."

Next, include your keyword in the meta description. This short paragraph appears below the title in search results and serves as a mini-ad for your article. While it doesn't directly influence your ranking, a well-crafted description with your keyword can boost your click-through rate, which is a positive signal for Google.

When it comes to the body of your article, your H1 heading (the main title) should contain the keyword. Use H2 subheadings for major sections and H3s for subsections to create a clear hierarchy in your content.


### Common SEO Mistakes vs. What Actually Works

Here is where most blog posts fall apart. These are not theoretical — I see these mistakes on sites every single week:

| ❌ Mistake | ✅ What to Do Instead |
|---|---|
| Keyword in title but nowhere else | Place keyword naturally in H1, first paragraph, one H2, and meta description |
| One 2,000-word post covering ten subtopics | Ten focused posts, each targeting one specific question |
| Writing for Google first | Write for the reader first; structure for Google second |
| Ignoring featured snippet formatting | Answer questions in 40–60 words directly, then expand below |
| No internal links | Link to 2–4 related posts per article, using descriptive anchor text |
| Generic meta description | Write it as an ad: include the keyword, a benefit, and a call to action |

### Keyword Placement: The Exact Spots That Matter

Once you've found your target keyword, here is exactly where it needs to appear — and where it does not:

**Must-have locations:**
1. **Title (H1):** The keyword should appear in your main title, ideally near the beginning
2. **First 100 words:** Google scans early content to understand the page topic
3. **At least one H2 subheading:** Use the keyword or a close variation
4. **Meta description:** Not a ranking factor directly, but improves click-through rates
5. **Image alt text:** Describe images using keyword-relevant terms where natural

**Where to avoid:**
- Forced repetitions mid-sentence that read awkwardly
- Footer text and sidebar text (Google ignores these for relevance signals)
- Stuffing in captions where it makes no sense

A good rule of thumb: if you're thinking about the keyword while writing the sentence, it will come across as forced. Write naturally, then review once to confirm it appears in the right structural spots.

### Internal Linking: The Underrated Ranking Signal

Most new bloggers treat internal links as an afterthought. That's a mistake. Google uses internal links to understand your site's topical structure and to pass PageRank between your pages.

For every article you publish, link to at least two other articles on your site using **descriptive anchor text** — not generic phrases like "click here" or "read more."

**Bad:** [Read more here](/blog/google-search-console-not-showing-data)

**Good:** [fix Google Search Console not showing data](/blog/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes)

The anchor text tells Google what the linked page is about. Over time, a web of well-structured internal links turns individual articles into a powerful topical cluster that ranks on multiple related searches.

Also consider the **hub-and-spoke model**: one comprehensive pillar post (like this one) links out to detailed spoke articles on subtopics, and each spoke links back to the hub. This is how tech blogs like this site structure their SEO strategy across categories like [website setup](/blog/how-to-add-your-website-to-google-search-step-by-step-guide).

### E-E-A-T: How Google Decides to Trust Your Content

Since the 2023 Helpful Content Update, Google has been ruthless about rewarding content that demonstrates real **Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness**. Here is what that actually means in practice:

**Experience:** Share personal observations. Instead of "studies show," write "when I tested this on my own site" or "after running this on 30 client articles." Specificity signals real experience.

**Expertise:** Demonstrate you understand the nuance. Anyone can write "use keywords" — an expert explains *which* type of keyword intent (informational, navigational, commercial, transactional) matters for each post format.

**Authoritativeness:** Cite credible sources. Link to Google's official search documentation, established research, or first-party data rather than other blog posts that are also citing other blog posts.

**Trustworthiness:** Be honest about limitations. If a strategy might not work for highly competitive niches (like it says in the limitations section above), say so. Google rewards accuracy over hype.

The fastest way to build E-E-A-T is to write articles where you've personally done the thing — not just researched it. A post about fixing a specific Windows update error you actually encountered and solved will consistently outrank a post written by someone summarizing forum threads.

### How Long Should Your Blog Post Be?

The honest answer: as long as it needs to be to fully answer the search query — and no longer.

That said, here are realistic benchmarks based on search intent:

- **News and current events:** 400–700 words
- **How-to guides:** 1,200–2,000 words
- **Comparison posts:** 1,500–2,500 words
- **Definitive / pillar guides:** 3,000–5,000 words

Google has explicitly stated that word count alone is not a ranking factor. A 600-word article that perfectly answers a specific question will outrank a padded 3,000-word article every time. The goal is **completeness**, not length.
