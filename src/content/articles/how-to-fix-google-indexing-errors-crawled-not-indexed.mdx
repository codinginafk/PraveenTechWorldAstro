Have you ever logged into Google [Search Console](/articles/google-search-console-not-showing-data-8-fixes) and felt your heart sink upon seeing "Crawled - currently not indexed" or "Discovered - currently not indexed" next to your URLs? Yeah, it's a bummer. It's like Google knows your page exists but just isn't interested in showing it in search results.

These two messages are some of the most common and often confusing indexing issues you'll encounter in [Search Console](/blog/how-to-add-your-website-to-google-search-step-by-step-guide). 

## What Each Status Actually Means

Let's break it down a bit:

**Discovered - currently not indexed** means that Google found your URL - probably through your sitemap or some links - but hasn't crawled it yet. Think of it like your page is in line waiting for its turn to be checked out.

This can happen for a few reasons:
- Your site might have a lot of pages, and Google is pacing itself.
- If your site is new, it may have a limited crawl budget.
- Google could have just found the URL and hasn't gotten around to it yet.

On the other hand, **Crawled - currently not indexed** means that Googlebot visited the page, read what it had to say, but decided not to include it in the index. This one's a bit more serious because it's a conscious choice by Google to skip your page.

Some common reasons for this include:
- The content is too thin or of low quality.
- There's duplicate content with no added value.
- Technical issues that make the page seem broken to Googlebot.
- Not enough internal links to signal the page's importance.

If you're just starting out with [Search Console](/articles/how-to-fix-sitemap-errors-in-google-search-console), you might want to check out the [GSC setup guide](/blog/how-to-add-your-website-to-google-search-step-by-step-guide) to learn how to verify your site and submit your sitemap.

## Step 1: Check If the Page Should Be Indexed

Before diving into fixes, let's ask the big question: should this page really be in Google's index?
Here are some page types that Google usually excludes on purpose:
- Thin affiliate pages without original content.
- Tag or category pages that duplicate existing content.
- Pages with noindex tags (you can check your HTML for `<meta name="robots" content="noindex">`).
- Pages blocked by robots.txt.
- Duplicate versions of other pages.

If your page fits any of these categories, the fix isn't really about technical adjustments; it's more about strategy. You might either need to enhance the page to justify indexing or accept that Google is making the right decision here.

## Step 2: Use the URL Inspection Tool

Now, let's open Google Search Console and drop the affected URL into the URL Inspection tool at the top of the page. This tool gives you a peek into what Google sees.

Check these details:
- **Coverage:** This tells you the current status and any detected issues.
- **Sitemap:** This shows whether Google found the URL through your sitemap.
- **Crawled page:** This shows the HTML Googlebot received; compare this to what a visitor would see.
- **Indexing allowed:** This confirms there are no noindex tags or robots.txt blocks.

If the "Crawled page" shows incomplete or broken HTML, it might indicate JavaScript rendering issues that prevented Googlebot from seeing the full content. 

## Step 3: Fix the Most Common Causes

### Thin Content
Google prefers substantial content to justify indexing. If you have pages with only 200 words of generic text, they might get overlooked. Adding real value - be it original research, step-by-step guides, or unique data - can really help.

### Internal Linking
If your pages have few or no internal links, they may signal to Google that they aren't important. Try adding contextual internal links from your popular pages. Each link helps boost authority and tells Google the page matters.

For more on this, the [SEO checklist for beginners](/blog/technical-seo-checklist-for-beginners-fix-these-issues-to-rank-better) goes over the basics of structuring your site for better indexing.

### Duplicate Content
If your page says the same thing as another page on your site, Google picks one version and leaves the others out. Using canonical tags can help tell Google which version is the original. It might also be worth consolidating similar pages into a single, comprehensive resource.

### Crawl Budget Issues
If your site has hundreds or thousands of pages, Google may not crawl all of them every time. So, it's a good idea to prioritize your most important pages in your sitemap and ensure internal links point to the pages that matter.

Google's crawl budget is limited, so it's best not to waste it on low-value pages. The [technical SEO checklist](/blog/technical-seo-checklist-for-beginners-fix-these-issues-to-rank-better) dives deeper into crawl budget and other technical fixes.

## Step 4: Request Indexing

Once you've made some fixes, go back to the URL Inspection tool and request indexing. Just click "Request Indexing," and Google will add your URL back to its crawl queue.

Keep in mind, this process isn't instant. Depending on your site's authority and crawl frequency, it can take days or even weeks for indexing to happen. But hey, requesting it manually is definitely faster than waiting for Google to rediscover your page.

## Step 5: Monitor the Index Coverage Report

After you've requested indexing for your URLs, keep an eye on the Index Coverage report in Search Console over the next few weeks. Look for:
- A decrease in error count.
- An increase in valid pages.
- Specific error types that might need more attention.

The report updates daily. If you don't see any changes after two weeks, it might be time to revisit the page content and internal links - something might still be blocking indexing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take for Google to index a page after requesting indexing'**
A: It can take anywhere from a few days to a few weeks. New sites with low authority might take longer. If nothing changes after two weeks, there might be quality or technical issues that need fixing first.

**Q: Can I fix all index coverage errors at once'**
A: Some fixes can be applied site-wide (like improving overall content quality or addressing robots.txt), but each URL might have its own unique issues. It's best to start with your most important pages and work through them one at a time.

**Q: Does submitting a sitemap fix indexing errors'**
A: A sitemap helps Google discover your URLs, but it doesn't guarantee indexing. Google still evaluates each page for quality and relevance before deciding whether to index it. So, a sitemap without quality content won't solve the issue.

**Q: What is the difference between "Excluded" and "Error" in Index Coverage'**
A: "Excluded" means Google intentionally skipped the page (which can be correct, like with duplicate content). "Error" means Google tried but failed, usually due to technical issues. "Excluded" pages might be fine; "Error" pages need fixing.

**Q: Will deleting and recreating a page help with indexing'**
A: Rarely. Google remembers the URL, so if the content was previously rejected, a new version with the same URL is likely to face the same outcome. It's better to improve the content at the existing URL instead of starting over.

## How Indexing Works for New Domains

If your domain is under 3 months old, encountering indexing errors is pretty common. Google tends to be cautious with new sites. It crawls a few pages, assesses quality, and waits before crawling more.

I've noticed this pattern myself. In the first two weeks, Google indexed 3 out of 20 submitted URLs. By week four, it was 15 out of 30. And by week eight, most of my new articles were getting indexed within days.

The takeaway? Google needs time to trust your site. Indexing errors on a new domain aren't always a sign of technical problems; sometimes the crawler is just pacing itself.

## When to Worry and When to Wait

Not every indexing error warrants immediate action. Some might resolve on their own as your site gains authority.

**Wait if:**
- Your domain is under 2 months old.
- The error is "Discovered - currently not indexed" (this often resolves within weeks).
- Only a small percentage of your pages have errors.
- The affected pages aren't your most important content.

**Fix if:**
- Your domain is 6+ months old with persistent errors.
- The error is "Crawled - currently not indexed" (this indicates Google evaluated and rejected the page).
- Most of your pages have the same error.
- Your most important pages (like your homepage or cornerstone articles) are affected.

I keep track of this with a simple spreadsheet. Each Monday, I note the total indexed count and error count. If errors decrease week over week, I don't worry. If they stay flat or increase, I dig a little deeper.

## Requesting Indexing the Right Way

When I find a page that should be indexed but isn't, I don't rush to request indexing right away. First, I check:

1. Does the page have at least 800 words of original content?
2. Does it have internal links from at least 2-3 other pages on my site?
3. Is there a clear path from the homepage to this page (within 3 clicks)'
4. Does the page load in under 3 seconds?
5. Is there a sitemap entry for this page?
If all five points check out, I go ahead and request indexing through the URL Inspection tool and then move on. If any of the checks fail, I address that issue first, wait for the next crawl, and then request indexing.

Requesting indexing repeatedly without fixing underlying issues doesn't help. Google keeps track of how many times you request indexing for the same URL, and asking too many times without improvements might signal to Google that you're trying to force something that doesn't meet its quality standards.

## Understanding the Index Coverage Report

The Index Coverage report in Google Search Console breaks down your site into four categories:

**Error.** Pages that Google tried to index but couldn't. These need immediate attention.

**Valid with warnings.** Pages that are indexed but have issues, such as missing meta descriptions or structured data errors.

**Valid.** Pages that have been successfully indexed. This number should ideally grow over time.

**Excluded.** Pages that Google intentionally skipped. This includes pages with noindex tags, redirects, 404 errors, and duplicate content. Most excluded pages aren't problems; they're Google correctly following your instructions.

I usually focus on the Error category. If errors are under 5% of total pages, I don't stress. But if they exceed 10%, I start investigating the patterns.
