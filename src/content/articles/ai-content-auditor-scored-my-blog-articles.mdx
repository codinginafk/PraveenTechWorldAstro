## My Blog's AI Content Got a Reality Check, It Scored 55/100

I knew my writing had problems, but seeing the actual score hit me like a ton of bricks. So I built a tool to measure exactly how much AI was sneaking into my work.

Like most writers these days, I use AI assistants - probably too much. DeepSeek, ChatGPT, Gemini - you name it, I've probably pasted my drafts into it. The result? A blog with 69 articles published in just under a month. The output was impressive, but the quality... not so much.

The writing had that telltale AI feel - too many em dashes, sentences that all sounded the same, that unmistakable robotic tone. I needed hard data to prove it, so I built a scoring tool.

### How the Scoring Works

My content grader checks each article across seven key areas:

1. **Sentence rhythm** - Looks at how much sentence length varies. Human writing naturally flows between short and long, while AI tends to be more uniform.
2. **Word variety** - Counts unique word usage. AI tends to repeat the same words.
3. **AI buzzwords** - Flags overused phrases like "delve deeper" that scream machine-written content.
4. **Transition words** - Tracks unnecessary "however" and "moreover" fillers.
5. **Dash overload** - Counts em dash addiction per 1, 000 words.
6. **Readability** - Uses standard Flesch score to check how easy it is to understand.
7. **Sentence structure** - Checks how much sentence patterns vary.

Each area gets a 0-100 score that combines into an overall grade. The tool also sends the text to DeepSeek for line-by-line feedback - these don't affect the score, just give specific fixes.

For technical SEO help, I've got a separate [technical SEO guide](/blog/technical-seo-checklist-for-beginners-fix-these-issues-to-rank-better). This tool focuses purely on writing quality.

### The Harsh Reality

After checking all 69 posts, the results hurt:

**Overall grades:**
- Average: 51/100
- Median: 55/100
- Best: 72
- Worst: 23

The patterns showed my bad habits:

- **52 articles** (76%) reused the same words constantly - "guide, " "fix, " "step" appeared way too often
- **51 posts** (74%) had robotic sentence rhythms with little variation
- **50 articles** contained 253 unnecessary em dashes total
- **462 specific issues** found, from filler sentences to entire AI-sounding paragraphs

The AI feedback didn't hold back, calling out empty transitions and redundant explanations that added words but no value.

### My Fixing Process

I built two helper scripts:

- **apply_fixes.py** - Matches suggested edits to original MDX files using five fallback methods (since the AI sees rendered HTML, not raw markdown)
- **clean_emdash.py** - Replaces em dashes with proper punctuation while keeping code blocks and frontmatter intact

After running both:

- **350 improvements** made across all articles
- **247 em dashes** replaced (mostly changing "**Header**, text" to "**Header**: text")
- **9 meta descriptions** fixed for missing punctuation
- **112 suggestions** ignored - either duplicates, mistakes, or frontmatter edits

About 25% of AI suggestions were off, but the rest caught issues I'd missed through multiple edits. The tool spotted repetitive phrasing I'd stopped noticing.

This isn't my first time automating fixes - my [server monitoring system](/blog/automated-server-health-checks-with-deepseek) and [database audit tool](/blog/non-developer-built-database-audit-script-with-deepseek) went through similar cycles.

### My Em Dash Problem

The most embarrassing find? 253 unnecessary em dashes across 50 articles. One GA4 guide had 18 alone.

The fixes were straightforward:

- **Bold headers** now use colons instead of dashes
- **Side thoughts** use actual parentheses
- **Sentence continuations** get proper punctuation

The cleaned-up versions read more intentionally - dashes became deliberate rather than automatic.

### What I Learned

This experiment taught me more about my writing than any editor could. Before seeing the numbers, I'd have guessed my articles averaged 70-75. The actual 55 average was eye-opening.

The seven metrics revealed patterns I couldn't see. You can't spot vocabulary repetition while writing - but the tool tracks every word across every sentence.

Of the 462 suggested fixes, I made 350. That 25% rejection rate represents keeping my voice while accepting valid criticism.

"Write better" is vague. "Boost your lexical diversity above 0.35" gives me something concrete to work on. Now I run every new post through the auditor before publishing.
