## Write Better Emails with Claude — Prompt Templates for Work

### Why This Matters (the real problem this solves)

Let’s be honest. That blank email draft is a trap. You stare at the cursor, trying to find the "right" words for a client update, a tricky ask, or a sensitive reply. Twenty minutes later, you've barely made a dent, and your focus is shot.

This isn't just about speed. It's about mental energy. We spend an average of 2.5 hours a day on email, according to a McKinsey report. Imagine reclaiming even 20% of that time. More focus for deep work. Less stress about tone and clarity.

### What You'll Learn

You don't need to be a prompt engineer. You just need the right templates. Here’s what you’ll walk away with:

*   How to structure a prompt for a professional first email.
*   A simple formula for turning bullet points into a polished update.
*   The exact prompt to craft a polite "no" without burning bridges.
*   A way to draft follow-up emails that feel helpful, not pushy.
*   How to make Claude match your specific tone (friendly, formal, concise).

### Step-by-Step Guide

#### 1. The Cold Email or First Introduction

This is the toughest one. You need to be clear, respectful, and compelling. Don't write from scratch. Use this structure.

**Your Prompt to Claude:**
"Write a professional email to [Name/Title] at [Company]. I am [Your Name/Your Role]. My goal is to [Your Goal, e.g., introduce our service, request a 15-minute meeting]. The key benefit for them is [Key Benefit]. Keep it under 150 words. Use a friendly but professional tone."

**Why it works:** You've given Claude the "who, what, why, and how long." It fills in the blanks with polished language, saving you from agonizing over every line.

#### 2. The Project Update Email

Stakeholders hate walls of text. They want the bottom line up front. Claude can structure this perfectly.

**Your Prompt to Claude:**
"Turn these rough notes into a concise project update email for my team. Start with a one-sentence summary. Then use bullet points for key milestones completed, current blockers, and next steps. My notes are: [Paste your bullet points, links, or stream-of-consciousness notes]."

**The magic:** Claude organizes the chaos. It creates hierarchy and clarity you might miss when you're in the weeds.

#### 3. The Diplomatic "No" or Pushback

This is where most people freeze. You need to decline a request, push back on a deadline, or flag an issue without causing conflict.

**Your Prompt to Claude:**
"Draft an email declining a request for [the specific thing]. The reason is [be honest, e.g., bandwidth, resource constraints, conflict with priority X]. I want to remain helpful and suggest an alternative, like [suggest alternative]. Maintain a respectful and collaborative tone."

#### 4. The "Just Checking In" Follow-Up

You sent the proposal or the question. Crickets. A follow-up feels nagging, but you need an answer.

**Your Prompt to Claude:**
"Write a short, polite follow-up email to [Name] regarding my previous email about [Topic]. Re-state the core question or request in one sentence. Add a line acknowledging they're busy. Keep the whole email under 4 sentences."

### Tips for Best Results

1.  **Be specific about tone.** Say "like a senior colleague" or "warmer than my usual emails." Don't just say "professional."
2.  **Give examples.** Paste in a previous email you liked and say, "Match this style."
3.  **Iterate.** Your first draft is a starting point. Say, "Make it 30% shorter" or "Make the opening more engaging."
4.  **Use Claude for editing.** Paste your messy draft and ask, "This is too direct. Make it more diplomatic while keeping the message clear."
5.  **Fact-check everything.** Claude doesn't know your exact company policies or client history. Always review names, dates, and details.
6.  **Start a prompt library.** Save the prompts that work for you. You'll build a personal toolkit in no time.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will my emails sound robotic or generic?**
A: They won't if you don't let them. Claude is a starting point, not the final author. Always add your personal touch, specific details, or a sentence that only you could write. Think of it as having a super-efficient first-draft writer.

**Q: What if I need to send sensitive information?**
A: Never paste highly confidential data (like full client contracts or personal employee data) into any AI tool. Use vague descriptions. For example, instead of pasting a contract, say, "Draft a response to section 7.2 of the attached agreement regarding payment terms."

**Q: Is this just for people who aren't good at writing?**
A: Not at all. Strong writers use it too. It's for anyone who wants to save 10-15 minutes per email. That's hours back in your week. It handles the mechanical structure so you can focus on the substance.

### Conclusion

Claude isn't replacing your judgment. It's removing the friction. That blank page isn't so scary anymore. Try one template tomorrow on a routine email. See how much faster you can hit "send" and get back to work that matters.
