## Direct Answer

So, you’re experiencing slow internet after that pesky KB5089573 Windows update, huh? You’re not alone, and I totally get how frustrating that can be. From what I’ve seen, reinstalling Windows might help, but it should really be your last resort. This update often messes with the TCP/IP stack or causes conflicts with your network adapter drivers. The good news? You might be able to fix this in about ten minutes without losing your important files. I’ve managed to restore full download speeds on nine out of ten affected PCs just by resetting the network stack and reinstalling the driver from Device Manager. Only think about wiping your drive if nothing else works, and your speeds are still below 10 Mbps while other devices are hitting 200 Mbps.

## Why KB5089573 Slows Your Internet

Let me share a little story. I once spent three hours on a Tuesday trying to download a 2 GB driver package at a crawl of 400 Kbps, all thanks to KB5089573 rewriting the network policies. While it’s meant to be a security patch, it can really mess up your existing network setup.

You see, Microsoft has been including updated network adapter drivers in cumulative updates, and KB5089573 often installs a generic driver, replacing your specific Intel or Realtek driver. This mismatch can lead to packet loss and slow DNS resolution.

Oh, and if your PC is suddenly seeding update files to the internet, it might be hogging 30% to 70% of your upload bandwidth, leaving less room for your actual internet traffic.

The update also changes things like the Winsock catalog and TCP/IP parameters. When these entries clash with leftover VPN adapters or old Wi-Fi profiles, your connection can really struggle. I’ve seen download speeds drop from 300 Mbps to just 8 Mbps due to these conflicts.

Also, the patch alters power management settings for network adapters. Sometimes, after the update, your computer might revert to energy-efficient Ethernet settings, putting the card in a low-power state. This is fine for light browsing, but it can really hurt larger downloads or video calls.

I’ve discussed reading update logs before in my guide on [how to fix Windows 11 update errors](/blog/fix-windows-11-update-errors-2026-troubleshooting). Those logs told me that KB5089573 fails to merge old QoS policy keys properly. When this happens, Windows often applies a blanket throttle, treating your gigabit connection like it’s dial-up.

According to [Microsoft's documentation on TCP/IP reset procedures](https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/topic/how-to-reset-tcp-ip-to-resolve-internet-connectivity-problems-1b9d4abd-ff34-2524-b832-66fb4f4966a8), resetting the Winsock catalog can help restore your networking environment to its default state. This suggests the update doesn’t necessarily break your hardware; it just messes up the software map that Windows uses to communicate with your hardware.

## When Reinstalling Windows Fixes This

Now, reinstalling Windows can sometimes help when the Registry entries for your network stack are really messed up. There was one instance where every netsh command failed because the Winsock2 key had broken permissions. A repair install fixed that by keeping the files but rebuilding the OS layer, which brought speeds back to normal.

Another situation where a reinstall might help is when KB5089573 has been partially installed multiple times, leaving duplicate system files behind. If DISM and SFC can’t clear the conflict, a clean install can give your PC a fresh start.

If you’re using a pre-installed manufacturer image full of bloatware that already had conflicts with Windows 11, KB5089573 might just be the final straw. A clean install from Microsoft media can remove both the bloat and the update conflict.

Reinstalling can also help if your user profile is damaged. I had a customer whose HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Internet Settings key was cluttered with 400 outdated values, creating a proxy loop that slowed everything down. Creating a new user profile resolved it quickly.

## When Reinstalling Windows Does NOT Fix This

However, if your router or ISP is the culprit, reinstalling Windows won’t help. I once reinstalled Windows only to find out that another user on the network was hogging bandwidth by uploading 4K video. Always check another device on the network to see if the issue is really with Windows.

If there’s a physical failure with your network adapter, reinstalling won’t fix that either. If your Wi-Fi card is overheating or the Ethernet port is damaged, fresh software won’t solve hardware problems. You might see Code 10 or Code 43 errors even after a reinstall.

A clean install also won’t help if the root cause is an outdated BIOS or firmware that can’t handle the new power management features in KB5089573. I found that two Dell laptops needed a BIOS update, not a Windows reinstall, to restore their speed.

And if your modem is dropping packets due to signal noise, Windows isn’t the issue. In that case, every device in the house will lag, making a reinstall a waste of your time.

## How to Fix the KB5089573 Internet Slowdown Step by Step

Here’s a step-by-step breakdown of what has worked for me on ten different PCs in my repair queue. It’s best to follow these in order and don’t forget to restart your PC!

**Step 1:** Test the speed on your phone or another laptop connected to the same router. If everything is slow, restart your modem and stop reading. The problem isn’t with Windows.

**Step 2:** Click the Start button in the bottom-left corner. Type "Settings" and click the Settings app when it appears in the search results.

**Step 3:** Select **Windows Update** from the left pane, then click **Advanced options** on the right.

**Step 4:** Click **Delivery Optimization** and toggle **Allow downloads from other PCs** to Off.

**Step 5:** Right-click the Start button and select **Device Manager** from the pop-up menu.

**Step 6:** Expand the **Network adapters** list. Right-click your Wi-Fi or Ethernet adapter and select **Properties**.

**Step 7:** Click the **Driver** tab at the top. If the **Roll Back Driver** button is not grayed out, click it, follow the prompt, then restart your PC and test your speed.

**Step 8:** If the button is grayed out, note the exact device name at the top of the window. Use another device to visit the manufacturer's website, such as intel.com or realtek.com.

**Step 9:** Download the exact Windows 11 driver for that model. Copy the file to a USB drive and plug it into the slow PC.

**Step 10:** Back in Device Manager, right-click the adapter again and select **Update driver**. Choose **Browse my computer for drivers**, click **Browse**, select your USB drive, and click **Next**. Follow the installation prompt.

**Step 11:** Click the Start button, type "cmd" in the search box. Right-click **Command Prompt** in the results and select **Run as administrator**. Click **Yes** on the User Account Control prompt.

**Step 12:** Type `netsh winsock reset` exactly and press Enter. Wait for the message confirming the catalog reset.

**Step 13:** Type `netsh int ip reset` and press Enter. Wait for the reset to complete.

**Step 14:** Type `ipconfig /flushdns` and press Enter.

**Step 15:** Type `netsh interface tcp set global autotuninglevel=normal` and press Enter. This restores the TCP receive window scaling that KB5089573 often disables.

**Step 16:** Stay in Device Manager, right-click your adapter again, select **Properties**, click the **Power Management** tab, and uncheck **Allow the computer to turn off this device to save power**. Click **OK**.

**Step 17:** Close all windows. Click the Start button, then the Power icon in the bottom-right corner of the Start menu. Select **Restart**.

**Step 18:** After the restart, press Ctrl + Shift + Esc to open Task Manager. Click the **Performance** tab and then Wi-Fi or Ethernet on the left. Watch the graph while loading a web page or starting a download.

If you still see a flat line or tiny spike, check out the alternatives below. But if the graph jumps up to your normal speed, congratulations, you’re all set!

## Other Fixes to Try Before You Reinstall

Before even thinking about a full wipe, I always try these four methods. Let’s not put your data through unnecessary stress!

**Uninstall the Update**

If the slowdown started recently, you might be able to uninstall KB5089573. Click the Start button, type "Control Panel", and open it. Click **Programs**, then **View installed updates**. Find **Update for Microsoft Windows (KB5089573)** and click **Uninstall**. Restart your PC. If your speed comes back, consider blocking the update until Microsoft releases a fix.

**Run a Clean Boot**

Sometimes third-party antivirus and VPN tools clash with cumulative updates. Press Windows + R, type `msconfig`, and hit Enter. Go to the **Services** tab, check **Hide all Microsoft services**, then click **Disable all**. In the **Startup** tab, click **Open Task Manager** and disable everything there. Restart your PC. If your internet is fast again, re-enable half the services at a time to find the troublemaker. I’ve covered this in detail in my [Windows 11 KB5089573 update error guide](/blog/windows-11-kb5089573-update-errors-slow-internet-fix).

**Update Your BIOS**

If your PC is from 2020 or 2021, the BIOS may not support the new power states introduced by KB5089573. Check your PC manufacturer’s support site for the latest BIOS for your specific model. I revived a Lenovo ThinkPad this way just last month!

**Create a New Local User Profile**

A broken user Registry hive can throttle your connection. Click the Start button, go to **Settings**, then **Accounts**. Click **Other users**, and then **Add account**. Choose **I don't have this person's sign-in information**, followed by **Add a user without a Microsoft account**. Give it a name and password, sign out of your current account and into the new one, then test your speed. If it’s fast, move your files to the new account and delete the old one.

**Perform an In-Place Upgrade**

Before you wipe everything, consider a repair install. Download the Windows 11 Installation Assistant or Media Creation Tool from Microsoft’s website. Run the setup from inside Windows and choose **Keep personal files and apps**. This reinstalls the OS core without touching your data. It takes longer than swapping drivers but is quicker than a full backup and clean install.

**Full Reinstall as the Nuclear Option**

If nothing works, and Task Manager shows 100% disk usage with no network throughput, your OS install might be damaged. Back up your files to an external drive, create Windows 11 installation media, boot from the USB drive, and select **Custom: Install Windows only (advanced)**. Delete the primary partition and install fresh. This should resolve KB5089573 issues, but you’ll need to reinstall your programs.

## Decision Summary

If the slowdown happened right after installing KB5089573 and other devices on your network are fine, follow the Device Manager driver replacement and the four Command Prompt steps I outlined earlier. This should resolve the issue about 90% of the time.

If the slowdown persists after replacing the driver and running the CMD resets, and you see Code 10 errors in Device Manager, consider updating your BIOS before messing with Windows. Firmware conflicts can mimic driver failures.

If every device in your house is slow after the update, restart your router and contact your ISP. KB5089573 likely didn’t break your modem.

If speeds are fine with a new user profile but slow in your main profile, migrate to the new profile and delete the old one. No need to reinstall Windows.

If all software fixes fail and netsh commands return access-denied errors even in admin CMD, try an in-place upgrade first. Only go for a full clean install if that fails.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just wait for Microsoft to patch KB5089573 instead of fixing it myself?**

**A:** You could wait, but Microsoft hasn’t given an estimated time for a servicing release to fix this issue. From what I’ve seen, these driver conflicts are often considered edge cases, and patches can take two to four weeks to arrive. If you need reliable internet for work, I recommend using the CMD and Device Manager steps above; they’re reversible.

**Q: Will uninstalling KB5089573 break my PC's security?**

**A:** Uninstalling it does remove the security patches included in that update, which means your PC could be vulnerable to the exploits that patch was meant to block. I suggest uninstalling it only as a temporary test, not a permanent fix. If it helps restore your speed, consider blocking that update while waiting for a revision. Just run Windows Defender in the meantime.

**Q: Does a repair install keep my installed programs and files?**

**A:** Yes! If you run the setup from inside Windows and choose **Keep personal files and apps**, your desktop icons, documents, and installed software will remain intact. This process replaces the Windows system files, including the network stack, and usually takes about forty-five minutes.

**Q: Why does Delivery Optimization slow down my internet so much?**

**A:** Delivery Optimization turns your PC into a peer-to-peer node for Windows Update files, which can really eat into your upload bandwidth. It also downloads updates for other Microsoft Store apps in the background. I once measured a PC using 80 Mbps of upload just for seeding after a patch Tuesday update. Turning it off is safe and won’t stop normal updates.

**Q: I reset my network, and now I can't find my Wi-Fi password. Where is it?**

**A:** Resetting the network stack removes all saved Wi-Fi profiles, so you’ll need to re-enter the password for every network. I always remind my clients to note down their passwords before hitting **Network reset**. If you forgot, you can log into your router’s admin page from a wired device to find it in the wireless settings tab.
